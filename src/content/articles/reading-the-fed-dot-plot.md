Every quarter, the Federal Open Market Committee releases what's formally called the Summary of Economic Projections. Buried inside is a chart — the dot plot — that shows where each anonymous FOMC member thinks the federal funds rate will be at the end of each of the next few years and over the long run.

Markets love it. Financial media dissects it for days. Traders move positions based on where the dots clustered.

Here's the thing: the dot plot has a poor track record as a forecasting tool.

## What It Actually Shows

Each dot represents one FOMC member's best guess about the appropriate rate level at year-end, given how they expect the economy to evolve. It is not a commitment. It is not a vote. It is a projection, made under conditions that will change, by people who openly acknowledge that forecasting is hard.

The Fed itself includes a large uncertainty range around the median projection — a cone of plausible outcomes that typically spans hundreds of basis points. That uncertainty range rarely makes headlines. The median dot does.

## The Track Record

Go back and compare historical dot plots to what actually happened. In 2021, the median dot suggested rates would stay near zero through 2023. By late 2022, rates were rising at the fastest pace in forty years. In 2018, the dots projected four rate hikes for 2019. There was one cut.

This isn't a criticism of the Fed. Forecasting monetary policy over a multi-year horizon is genuinely difficult. The economy is not a machine with knowable inputs and predictable outputs. The problem is that markets keep treating the dot plot as if it were.

## What to Watch Instead

The dot plot is still useful — just not for the reasons most traders use it.

What it tells you is the *distribution* of views inside the committee. A tight cluster of dots signals consensus; a wide scatter signals disagreement. Disagreement matters because it tells you how sensitive future policy is to incoming data. When dots are spread across 75 basis points, a couple of hot inflation prints could shift the median materially. When they're tightly packed, there's more inertia.

The language in the chair's press conference matters more than any single dot. Pay attention to how the Fed describes uncertainty — "we believe," "we expect," "data will guide" — because that language tells you how much optionality they're preserving.

## The Honest Read

The dot plot is a snapshot of what a group of smart economists thought was likely on a specific day, using data that was current as of that day. By the time the next quarterly release arrives, the world has changed. The dots move, sometimes dramatically.

Use it as a rough prior, not a roadmap. And when someone on financial television says "the Fed is signaling X number of cuts this year," remember: they're reading dots, not reading the future.
