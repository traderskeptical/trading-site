For decades, the yield curve inversion was the closest thing macroeconomics had to a reliable leading indicator. When short-term Treasury yields climbed above long-term yields — when the 2-year rate exceeded the 10-year — a recession typically followed within twelve to eighteen months.

The logic was sound. Short-term rates reflect current Fed policy. Long-term rates reflect expectations about future growth and inflation. When investors expect conditions to deteriorate, they buy long bonds, pushing long yields down. When the Fed is tightening hard, short yields rise. The crossing of those lines historically signaled that the market expected tighter policy to slow the economy into contraction.

Then 2022 happened.

## The Inversion That Wouldn't Resolve

The 2-year/10-year spread inverted in the spring of 2022 and stayed inverted for over two years — one of the deepest and longest inversions in modern history. By the conventional playbook, a severe recession should have materialized. GDP did contract in two consecutive quarters in 2022, briefly meeting one common definition. But unemployment stayed low, consumer spending held up, and the labor market remained tight well into 2024.

The signal that had predicted every recession since the 1970s appeared to misfire. Financial media cycled through explanations: "this time is different," "soft landing," "structural shift." Most of those explanations were reached backward from the outcome.

## What Actually Changed

Several structural factors complicated the relationship.

**The QE distortion.** Following the 2008 crisis, the Fed accumulated trillions in long-dated Treasuries through quantitative easing. That sustained demand artificially suppressed long yields for years, compressing the spread in ways that had nothing to do with recession expectations. The historical relationship was built on a market where private participants set long rates. That market was gone.

**Demographics and the savings glut.** An aging global population has pushed long-run demand for safe, long-duration assets steadily higher. Pension funds, insurance companies, and sovereign wealth funds structurally bid for long bonds regardless of the economic cycle. This creates a persistent downward pressure on long yields that didn't exist in the same form forty years ago.

**The starting point.** In most historical inversions, the curve inverted because long rates were falling toward short rates. In 2022, it inverted because short rates were rising rapidly toward long rates that were themselves elevated. The mechanism — and what it signals — may be different depending on which end of the curve is moving.

## What to Use Instead

The yield curve isn't worthless. It's still a useful piece of information about the relative price of risk across time horizons. But treating a single spread as a binary recession predictor — inversion equals recession coming — ignores everything else happening in the economy.

Combine it with credit spreads. High-yield spreads widening is a more real-time signal of stress in the real economy than anything Treasury yields do in isolation. Add the unemployment claims trend. Add leading indicators for manufacturing. The yield curve is one data point, not a verdict.

## The Honest Read

Economic indicators change their predictive value as the economy they're measuring changes. The 2022 inversion doesn't mean the yield curve never matters — it means that a relationship calibrated against an economy with different monetary policy, demographics, and capital flows needs to be held more loosely than the textbook suggests.

If someone tells you the yield curve *always* signals recession, ask them when they last updated that always.
